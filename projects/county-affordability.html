County Affordability Analysis

Rigorous applied regression study investigating what county-level factors predict housing cost-to-income ratios across 2,762 U.S. counties — comparing OLS, ridge, and LASSO models with full diagnostics and interaction analysis.

Organization
STAT 420 — Methods of Applied Statistics, UIUC
Category
Statistical Analysis
Tags
RStatistics and Analytics

Background

Completed for STAT 420 (Methods of Applied Statistics) at UIUC. Dataset sourced from Kaggle, containing quality-of-life metrics across U.S. counties compiled from the Census Bureau, EPA, and USDA. The response variable is the mean cost-to-income ratio (AVG C2I) per county as of 2022.

Objective

Investigate which county-level variables most influence housing affordability using rigorous linear regression methodology — comparing multiple model families, diagnosing assumption violations, and arriving at a defensible final model with interpretable coefficients.

Outcome

Final model achieved adjusted R² of 0.507 and LOOCV RMSE of 13.70, combining stepwise selection, a quadratic unemployment term, and locale interaction effects. OLS outperformed both LASSO and ridge, as expected given n = 2,762 with limited overfitting risk.

Key Findings

Strongest predictor

Unemployment is by far the most significant variable — each 1pp increase is associated with ~7.4pp higher cost-to-income ratio, with a concave relationship that diminishes at high unemployment values.

Urban vs. rural asymmetry

Urban core counties (Locale 11) show the steepest unemployment-affordability slope (~22.6). The effect is roughly halved in non-urban locales — mid-size cities add only ~11.6pp per unemployment point.

School staffing as a proxy

Student-teacher rank is the second most consistent predictor — a 1,000-rank improvement in school staffing is associated with ~3.7pp lower AVG C2I, likely reflecting broader county wealth.

Model fit

Adjusted R² ≈ 0.51 on the full dataset, rising to 0.59 when 116 influential outliers are removed — suggesting the model is conservative rather than overfitted.

Notable outlier

Todd County, SD (AVG C2I = 287%) is the most extreme observation — a persistently impoverished, Native American-majority county where median income is $25,530 against a cost of living of $73,348.
