Cyclistic Bike-Sharing Analysis

Exploratory analysis comparing usage patterns between casual riders and annual members of a Chicago bike-sharing service, leading to actionable marketing strategy recommendations.

Background

Completed as the capstone project for the Google Data Analytics Professional Certificate. The scenario uses Cyclistic, a fictional bike-sharing company modeled on Chicago's real Divvy system, with the business question of how to convert casual riders into annual members.

Objective

Analyze how casual riders and annual members differ in their usage patterns — by time of day, day of week, trip duration, and station preferences — and turn those differences into targeted marketing recommendations.

Outcome

A published Kaggle notebook demonstrating the complete six-step data analysis workflow: Ask, Prepare, Process, Analyze, Share, Act. Clear visualizations and concrete recommendations grounded in behavioral differences between rider segments.

Key Findings

Usage patterns differ sharply by day

Casual riders show no strong weekday preference by ride count, but ride significantly longer on weekends, especially Sundays. Members ride slightly less on weekends but maintain consistent duration across all days.

Casual riders prefer electric bikes

While members show no meaningful bike type preference, casual riders overwhelmingly favor electric bikes — a clear lever for targeted marketing or inventory decisions.

High-duration outliers among casual riders

A subset of casual riders logs unusually long ride times — the most promising conversion targets, since they already demonstrate high engagement without a membership.

January 1st spike

Casual riders showed a notable peak on January 1st in both ride count and average duration. possibly driven by New Year's resolutions, suggesting a recurring seasonal marketing opportunity worth investigating across other months.

Recommendations

Target high-duration casuals directly

Reach out to casual riders who exceed a duration threshold via in-app or on-bike prompts explaining membership savings. They are already high-engagement users and the most likely to convert.

Design a leisure-focused membership tier

Current membership is structured for commuters. A weekend-oriented plan with longer individual ride allowances would better fit casual rider behavior and lower the conversion barrier.

Leverage electric bike preference

Center marketing campaigns around electric bikes, or offer membership bundles tied to electric bike access — directly addressing the feature casual riders already seek out.
